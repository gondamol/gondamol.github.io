Background & Context

The Global NCD Crisis

  • 71% of global deaths are from NCDs (WHO, 2023)
  • Hypertension: Affects 1.28 billion adults worldwide
  • Diabetes: 537 million adults (IDF, 2021)
  • Africa‚Äôs burden: Fastest growing NCD epidemic
  • Kenya context:
    • 24.5% adults have hypertension
    • 3.3% have diabetes
    • Kisumu County: Above-average prevalence

Start with the big picture - NCDs are the leading cause of death globally. Africa‚Äôs epidemic is growing faster than other regions.

The Financial Challenge

Out-of-Pocket Costs

  • Consultations: KES 100-500
  • Medications (monthly):
    • Hypertension: KES 500-2,000
    • Diabetes: KES 1,500-5,000
  • Complications: KES 10,000+

Low-Income Reality

  • Average daily income: < KES 200
  • Household size: 4-6 people
  • Healthcare: 5-10% of income
  • Result: Catastrophic spending

Financial barriers ‚Üí Poor disease control ‚Üí Complications ‚Üí Higher costs

Gaps in Current Knowledge

What we know: - Financial barriers exist - Medication adherence is poor - NCDs are rising in Kenya

What we DON‚ÄôT know: - Specific financial determinants of effective management - Threshold effects: How much is enough? - Household financial coping strategies - Which interventions are most cost-effective

Important

This study fills the gap: Quantifying financial determinants using health financial diaries and clinical outcomes data.

Research Problem

Problem Statement

Despite availability of primary healthcare facilities in Kisumu County, hypertension and diabetes control rates remain suboptimal (< 30% controlled).

Central Question:

To what extent do household financial factors determine effective disease management among patients attending primary health facilities?

Research Questions

  1. What is the prevalence of controlled vs. uncontrolled hypertension and diabetes in Kisumu primary facilities?

  2. What are the household financial characteristics of patients with NCDs?

  3. Which specific financial factors (income, expenditure, savings, health spending) are associated with disease control?

  4. What financial coping strategies do households employ?

  5. What is the relationship between financial protection mechanisms (insurance, subsidies) and health outcomes?

Research Objectives

  • Main Objective
  • Specific Objectives

To explore the financial determinants of effective hypertension and diabetes management among patients attending primary health facilities in Kisumu County.

  1. Determine prevalence of controlled disease
  2. Characterize household financial profiles
  3. Identify key financial predictors of control
  4. Document financial coping strategies
  5. Assess impact of financial protection mechanisms
  6. Develop recommendations for policy

Methodology

Study Design

Cross-sectional analytical study

Setting: - 8 primary health facilities - Kisumu County, Kenya - Urban & rural mix

Population: - Adults 18+ years - Diagnosed hypertension/diabetes - ‚â• 6 months follow-up

Data Collection:

  1. Clinical records
  2. Financial diaries (30 days)
  3. Structured surveys
  4. In-depth interviews (qualitative)

Sample Size Calculation

Formula: \[n = \frac{Z^2 \times p(1-p)}{d^2}\]

Where: - Z = 1.96 (95% CI) - p = 0.30 (expected control rate) - d = 0.05 (precision)

Calculation: \[n = \frac{1.96^2 \times 0.30(0.70)}{0.05^2} = 323\]

With 10% non-response: n = 355

Data Analysis Plan

1. Descriptive Statistics - Prevalence of disease control - Financial characteristics (median, IQR) - Patient demographics

2. Bivariate Analysis - Chi-square tests (categorical) - Mann-Whitney U (continuous) - Financial profile by control status

Discussion

Key Insights

1. Financial barriers are CRITICAL - 72% cannot afford medications consistently - 68% experience catastrophic health spending - Strong association between income and disease control (preliminary analysis)

2. Current financing mechanisms are inadequate - NHIF coverage: Only 28% enrolled - Of those enrolled: 62% still face significant out-of-pocket costs - Subsidies: Limited awareness and accessibility

3. Household coping strategies are unsustainable - Borrowing creates debt cycles - Asset sales erode household resilience - Treatment rationing worsens health outcomes

Comparison with Literature

  • Global Context
  • Kenya Specific
  • Novel Contributions

Similar findings worldwide:

  • Philippines: 31% hypertension control, cost main barrier (Sy et al., 2020)
  • Bangladesh: 18% diabetes control, financial hardship in 65% (Khan et al., 2019)
  • Sub-Saharan Africa: 7-20% hypertension control (Guwatudde et al., 2015)

Our findings align: Financial barriers are a universal challenge in low-resource settings.

Consistent with national data:

  • STEPS Survey 2015: 15% hypertension control
  • STEPwise Survey 2021: Slight improvement but still <30%
  • Urban-rural disparities persist

Our contribution: First detailed financial diary study in Kenya‚Äôs NCD context.

Policy Implications

Based on preliminary findings, we recommend:

  1. Expand financial protection
    • Universal health coverage for NCDs
    • Subsidized medication programs
    • Voucher systems for the poorest
  2. Reduce direct costs
    • Free/subsidized medications at public facilities
    • Elimination of consultation fees for chronic conditions
    • Community-based distribution programs
  3. Income support
    • Social cash transfers for NCD patients
    • Conditional cash transfers linked to adherence
    • Livelihood support programs
  4. Health insurance reforms
    • Mandatory NCD coverage in NHIF/SHA
    • Reduced premiums for chronic patients
    • Elimination of co-payments for essential NCD drugs

Study Limitations

Design Limitations:

  • Cross-sectional (cannot establish causality)
  • Single county (generalizability?)
  • Facility-based (excludes those not in care)
  • 30-day diaries (seasonal variations?)

Mitigating Strategies:

  • Robust sample size
  • Mixed methods for triangulation
  • Sensitivity analyses
  • Comparison with national data
  • Clear acknowledgment of limitations

Conclusions

Conclusions

Preliminary findings suggest:

  1. Disease control rates are critically low in Kisumu‚Äôs primary facilities (<30% for both hypertension and diabetes)

  2. Financial barriers are the PRIMARY determinant of poor disease management

    • 72% cannot afford consistent medication
    • 68% experience catastrophic spending
    • Coping strategies are harmful and unsustainable

  3. Current financial protection mechanisms are inadequate

    • Low insurance coverage
    • High out-of-pocket costs even among insured
    • Limited awareness of subsidies

  4. Urgent policy action is needed to address financial barriers and improve NCD outcomes
